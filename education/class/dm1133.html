October 24, 2017

Originially my idea for the midterm was going to be a "find your way out" sort of point and click game where you got locked in a dark attick. Then, it was going to show someone droppng a photo and it cracking in a way where the user could interact with the pieces. So, after discussing these ideas and getting both of them approved, I decided to change it. Welcome to Mouse Paint.

Mouse Paint is the name of a children's book by Ellen Stoll Walsh and used to be a favorite of mine. In it, there are mice that climb into jars of primary color paint and then dance in puddles of different colored paint to create secondary color paint. Covering yourself with paint is essentially a toddler's idea of a crazy good time, so the book also espoused the idea of painting on paper instead of yourself.

I was inspired by the book because of its straightforward storyline (mice go swimming in paint), it's nostalgia component (I think the arcade games in MAGNET bring that out in me), and more of all because of the easy way to integrate interactivity into the game (the player controls the mice and which puddles of paint they go into).

I explain all this because there are many aspects that seem out of place in the game without knowing of the book. Why mention the cat at all? Why only primary colors? Why is the art so bad and childish? And if I continue to work on it, I might change some of these aspects and find a way to combine pixel values or have the mice become browner with each puddle they pass through.

I hope to improve upon it in the future. While I made a lot of photos with the mice's feet changing color every time they passed through a new puddle, I found it slowed down the program to have all of those preloaded. I also would like to add a way for mice to leave footprints as they walk which change color, and while it was something that I worked on, I wasn't able to finish that aspect in time for the submission.

I decided to combine the two assignments into one program. This is a program where a ball bounces off the four walls of the screen and plays "Do" "Re" "Mi" or "Fa" from blackbear's song do re mi. November 7, 2017

I love the sound of rain, and so I wanted my project to reflect that story weather vibe but with an uplifting or almost haunting sound rather than the sound of thunder or the typical rain sound. I wanted it to feel melodic. I went into Garage Band and played notes on the Glass Bells Synthesizer. I isolated the notes into 8 different tracks and then muted them separately in order to get a full octave of bells.

My biggest problem has been actually playing the sounds. I tried really hard to sort them into an array and then call them in various ways, but I either got an error that the play() function didn't exist (even though it worked when I hardcoded a specific sound) or that the value was undefined. Ulitimately I had to change it so the sound that played reflected the x value of the droplet. The other problem I had was that I couldn't figure out how to implement the splice function so that the droplets were removed when they were no longer needed.

I have one more problem that I'm dealing with, which is why this is a bit late. I realized too late that because the droplets never got deleted, the sound kept getting called for each frame, which led to a cacophony of bells being played even when no droplets landed. That's a problem I'm still trying to figure out, but ulimately the program runs for now.

November 9, 2017

This program takes portrays all the named/recognized HTML colors from a JSON file and displays their color in a randomized spattering of circles.

It also shares the name of the color alongside the mouse when the mouse hovers over a certain color.

I set the background to default to a value of 200, though I realize that some colors would be harder to see on certain backgrounds. Because of this, I allowed the viewer to make the background color lighter or darker if they pressed the up or down arrow. This would allow for easier viewing of the dots and their names if the color was too similar to the shade it was on.

My improvements for the future would be actually organizing the dots in an informative pattern. My plan is to be able to press r, g, or b, and have the dots organize in a way that has the highest color value first. I just figured out how to get a certain value from the color about an hour ago, and because of that I havent implemented this yet. While I will first simply organize in order of value of the r, g, and b, values, my hope is that I will then do it on r g b ratio so that the colors most leaning toward that color would be first in line when summoned.

Final Project

I have created an Audio Visualizer.

This project consisted of many obstacles, mostly consisting of inconstant internet and coding roadblocks that led to reevaluating my code, my goals, and my entire concept. I originally planned to do a final that consisted of two APIs, but as I fell behind in my being able to fully understand the documentation and being able to access the APIs due to lack of internet initially over break, I changed my project. The next version of my final was an audio visualizer that would have the visuals of a 2d mode, a 3d mode, and a 2d/3d mode, with the audio modes of microphone, preset songs, and then the Spotify API. I realized with time that doing a WebGL canvas and a frequency spectrum was impossible, not only on the same screen but within the same file. I thought about perhaps working on two files separately - the 2d visualizer and the 3d visualizer. Then, snow hit Maine and our wifi became inconsistent and I gave up on the idea of a Spotify API addition. While I had planned for the idea of not having a Spotify API - the updated proposal I sent you mentioned a visualizer for user curated/combined sounds - I realized that that didn’t work with the general idea of the project. My final is something I can easily see myself casting to my TV as I do work, changing between the visual modes as I found myself desiring a new way of seeing the sounds I was playing. I wanted to have one, cohesive file that acted as a complete visualizer. With that in mind, I worked on and finished my final.

The ideas I came up with resulted from a mixture of demonstrating knowledge regarding what we have learned this year and other audio functions, a desire to represent music data in a fun and interesting way, and adapting ideas from other projects I found when searching for various functions or figuring out limitations. I wanted to make sure I played with functions I hadn’t used before, so I made sure to seek out a new library and new ways of doing things (ie, pushing push, pop, transform, and rotate instead of simply drawing a snowflake). I decided to use the scribble library for the play/pause button, the “sun,” and the snowflakes.

The core audio functions I have are an ability to play and pause a song, the ability to switch between songs, the ability to use all visualizations with a microphone, and the ability to drag and drop a song into the project and have it play, a function which I chose in replacement of the ever changing third audio functions I had previously wanted.
